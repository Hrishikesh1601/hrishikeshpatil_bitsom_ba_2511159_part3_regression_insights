Final Business Recommendation



Key Findings

From the analysis, I found that monthly sales are influenced by a combination of factors rather than a single variable. Marketing spend, customer footfall, average discount percentage, staff count, inventory availability, region and store type all showed some level of association with sales.

The final model also achieved an R-squared value of 0.8305, which means it explains about 83% of the variation in monthly sales. This suggests that the model is able to explain a large part of the sales performance using the available data.



My Recommendations

Based on the results, I would suggest the following:

- Continue investing in marketing because it has a positive relationship with monthly sales.
- Focus on increasing customer footfall through promotions, loyalty programs and better customer engagement.
- Ensure products are always available by maintaining good inventory levels.
- Maintain adequate staffing so that customers receive better service during busy periods.
- Compare the performance of different regions and store types to understand what works well and apply those practices to other stores.

These steps can help improve sales performance while supporting long-term business growth.



Points to Keep in Mind

Although the model provides useful insights, every coefficient should not be interpreted in isolation. Some variables may have a stronger influence on sales than others, and business decisions should always consider the overall picture instead of focusing on a single number.

The regression model is a helpful tool, but it should be used along with business knowledge and practical experience.



Limitations

This analysis is based only on the variables available in the dataset. In reality, many other factors can influence monthly sales, such as festivals, weather conditions, local competition, customer preferences and economic changes.

Since these factors were not included in the data, the model cannot explain every change in sales.



Regression Shows Association, Not Proof

One thing I learned during this project is that regression analysis shows how variables are related, but it does not prove that one factor directly causes another.

For example, marketing spend has a positive relationship with monthly sales, but that does not guarantee that increasing marketing spend alone will always increase sales. Other business conditions also affect the final outcome.

Because of this, the regression results should be treated as a guide for decision-making rather than absolute proof.



Conclusion

Overall, this project helped me understand how regression analysis can be used to study business performance and support decision-making. After comparing all the models, I found that the multiple regression model gave the most meaningful results because it included several important business factors together.

While no model can predict sales perfectly, the final model provides useful insights that management can use while planning marketing activities, managing inventory and improving overall store performance.
