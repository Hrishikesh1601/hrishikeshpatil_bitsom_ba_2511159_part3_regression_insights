Model Comparison



Model 1 – Simple Regression (Marketing Spend)

In the first model, I used monthly_sales as the dependent variable and marketing_spend as the independent variable.

I wanted to check whether spending more on marketing is associated with higher monthly sales. The results gave a basic idea about this relationship and were easy to understand. However, sales are affected by many other factors as well, so looking only at marketing spend does not give the complete picture.



Model 2 – Simple Regression (Footfall)

For the second model, I used footfall as the independent variable.

The idea was to see whether stores with more customer visits generally record higher sales. The results showed the relationship between footfall and sales, but again, customer visits alone cannot explain overall store performance. Other factors like discounts, inventory availability and store location also play an important role.



Model 3 – Multiple Regression

The third model included several business variables together, such as marketing spend, footfall, average discount percentage, staff count, inventory availability and the dummy variables created for region and store type.

Since this model considers several factors at the same time, it gives a much clearer picture of what is associated with monthly sales. It also helps understand the contribution of each variable while keeping the others constant, making the analysis more practical for business decisions.



Comparing the Models

Both simple regression models were helpful in understanding the impact of one variable at a time. They are easy to interpret and useful for getting an initial understanding of the data.

However, business performance is usually influenced by multiple factors working together. The multiple regression model captures these combined effects, making it more suitable for analysing monthly sales.

The final multiple regression model achieved an R-squared value of 0.8305, which means it explains about 83% of the variation in monthly sales. The Adjusted R-squared value of 0.8244 also indicates that the model performs well even after considering the number of variables included.


Final Model Selection

After comparing all three models, I selected the Multiple Regression Model as the final model for this project.

I chose this model because it looks at different business factors together instead of focusing on only one variable. In a real retail business, sales are influenced by marketing, customer visits, discounts, inventory and store characteristics at the same time. This model reflects that situation much better and therefore provides more meaningful business insights.



Conclusion

Overall, comparing different regression models helped me understand that relying on just one factor can sometimes be misleading. The multiple regression model gave the most balanced and reliable results, so I selected it as the final model.

At the same time, it is important to remember that regression analysis only shows the relationship between variables. It helps identify patterns in the data, but it does not prove that one factor directly causes changes in monthly sales.
